📋
Abstract This research programme proposes using latent diffusion models (LDM) to synthesise photorealistic wall-surface textures, generate diverse structural defects on demand, and close the simulation-to-real domain gap for automated visual inspection systems. The pipeline combines a surface-conditioned U-Net denoiser, a multi-modal defect controller (ControlNet-style), and an adversarial domain alignment module to produce inspection data that is both perceptually realistic and semantically accurate — enabling robust detector training without costly real-world defect collection.
Section 01

Core Idea: Diffusion for Wall-Surface Simulation

Diffusion models learn to reverse a noise process: given a wall image progressively corrupted into pure Gaussian noise over T timesteps, the model learns to denoise it back — and by starting from random noise with conditioning signals, it can generate any wall texture or defect type on demand.

🧱 Why Diffusion for Walls?

Traditional rendering engines produce geometrically correct but perceptually unconvincing surface textures — subtle features like micro-cracks, uneven paint sheens, efflorescence patterns, and shadow discontinuities across material boundaries are extremely hard to script by hand.

Diffusion models operate in a continuous latent space and are trained on millions of real-world wall images, so they automatically encode rich texture priors — and can be steered with text prompts, edge maps, depth cues, or defect masks to produce exactly the sample needed for inspection dataset augmentation.

The key insight: the denoising U-Net is a learnable prior over all physically plausible wall appearances, and conditioning allows controlled synthesis of rare but important failure modes.

Problem Decomposition

The overarching research challenge decomposes into three tightly coupled sub-problems, each addressed by a dedicated module in the shared diffusion backbone.

01
🧱

Photorealistic Surface Synthesis

Generate geometrically and photometrically accurate wall-surface images including varied materials (concrete, plaster, brick, tile, painted drywall), lighting conditions, perspective, and fine surface micro-textures that fool supervised classifiers trained on real data.

02
🔍

Defect Synthesis on Demand

Controllably insert realistic structural defects — cracks, efflorescence, delamination, spalling, mould growth, corrosion staining — at arbitrary locations and severity levels, paired with pixel-level segmentation masks for downstream detector supervision.

ControlNet Mask Inpainting Diffusion Severity Conditioning Auto-Mask Generation
03
🔄

Sim-to-Real Domain Adaptation

Bridge the distribution gap between synthetically generated and real-world inspection images using adversarial training, cycle consistency in latent space, and feature alignment so that detectors trained on synthetic data perform well on real inspection cameras.

Latent CycleGAN Domain Classifier Feature Alignment Loss Style Transfer
🔗
Why These Three Must Be Solved Jointly Solving P1 alone gives pretty images but no defect annotation. Solving P2 without P1 produces unrealistic defect backgrounds that detectors learn to ignore. Without P3, even perfect synthetic data fails to transfer — models latch onto synthetic rendering artefacts. A unified diffusion backbone with shared weights addresses all three simultaneously through multi-task conditioning and a single end-to-end training objective.

Section 04

Defect Synthesis — Taxonomy & Approach

Six canonical defect classes are modelled with dedicated conditioning pathways. Each class uses a class-conditioned inpainting approach where the defect region's latent is re-noised to a partial timestep t* and then denoised with the target defect embedded in the cross-attention context.

〰️
Structural Crack
Thin-line diffusion · Width controlled by σ param · Hairline to 5mm via severity embedding · Auto-vectorised mask from stroke input
🌿
Efflorescence
Reaction-diffusion texture prior · Salt-crystal distribution sampled from real SEM images · White bloom opacity 0–100%
🔴
Paint Delamination
Laplacian peeling boundary GAN · Multi-layer paint stack (top/base/substrate) revealed progressively · Curl edge simulation
🟣
Biological Growth
Fractal mold colony texture · Humidity & moisture metadata conditioning · Green / black / grey mould variants via text token
🔵
Surface Spalling
Voronoi fracture mask stitched into inpainting region · Depth discontinuity map as ControlNet hint · Exposed aggregate simulation
🟡
Corrosion Staining
Iron-oxide rebar stain simulation · Gravitational drip path computed from surface normal map · Colour from real stain colour LUT
⚠️
Rare Defect Augmentation In real inspection datasets, severe defects (severity 4–5) account for <2% of images. The diffusion model explicitly up-samples these using classifier-guidance towards high severity scores, enabling training set re-balancing without physical destruction testing. This directly addresses the long-tail problem in industrial AI.
Section 05

Domain Adaptation — Closing the Sim-to-Real Gap

Even photorealistic diffusion outputs contain subtle distribution shifts — sensor noise profiles, JPEG compression artefacts, colour response curves — that cause trained detectors to fail on real cameras. Three complementary techniques are applied.

⚙️ Latent CycleGAN

Two generators GS→R and GR→S operate in the VAE latent space (4× compressed), making cycle consistency affordable. An identity loss preserves defect morphology while transferring camera and lighting style. No paired data needed.

🎯 Domain-Adversarial Feature Alignment

Gradient Reversal Layer (GRL) is inserted between the U-Net bottleneck and a domain classifier. By maximising domain confusion at the feature level, the encoder learns domain-invariant representations — retaining defect semantics while discarding camera artefacts.

🔄 Differentiable Camera Augmentation

At inference, camera response functions (CRFs) sampled from a real-world camera CRF database are applied as differentiable post-processing layers. Combined with learned noise profiles and JPEG-Q augmentation, synthetic outputs are rendered to match specific target camera models used in deployment.

Section 06

Training Strategy

A staged, multi-objective training curriculum progressively integrates each module to avoid training instabilities from competing losses.

Phase 1 — Weeks 1–4
🏗 Pre-train Surface VAE
Train a KL-regularised VAE on a large-scale wall texture dataset (500K+ patches, 512×512px). Objective: reconstruction + KL divergence. Learn a compact 4-channel latent space that captures material microstructure, illumination gradients, and surface topology. Evaluate with FID and LPIPS on held-out real wall patches.
Phase 2 — Weeks 5–10
🧠 Pre-train LDM U-Net (Unconditional)
Pre-train the denoising U-Net on VAE latents with DDPM loss only (no conditioning). This bootstraps the denoiser's understanding of valid wall-surface latent manifolds before fine-grained conditioning is introduced. Scheduler: cosine noise schedule, T=1000. Warm up with low-res 64×64 latents, then scale to full resolution.
Phase 3 — Weeks 11–18
⚡ Fine-tune with Full Multi-Modal Conditioning
Introduce text conditioning (CFG dropout p=0.1), ControlNet branch (mask/edge), material and severity embeddings simultaneously. Frozen text encoder (T5-XL). Joint loss: Ldiffusion + λperceptual·LLPIPS + λmask·Lmask-align. Gradient accumulation over 4 GPUs.
Phase 4 — Weeks 19–24
🔄 Domain Adaptation Fine-tuning
Add Latent CycleGAN and GRL adversarial objectives with conservative λadv=0.1 (ramped up over 3 epochs to avoid mode collapse). Camera augmentation applied online. Evaluate with Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) between synthetic outputs and real inspection image sets from 3 target camera deployments.
